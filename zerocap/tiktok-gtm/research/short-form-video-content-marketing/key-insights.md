# Key Insights: What You Need to Know

Read this first. The rest of the research is reference material -- this is what actually changes how you think about content marketing for SpaceZero.

---

## TikTok is not the best channel anymore

Everyone defaults to "TikTok for short-form video." The data says otherwise. Owlcat Games tested the same creator content across platforms and found 85-90% of creators got better organic reach on Instagram Reels and YouTube Shorts than TikTok. One creator went from 425K views to 6K views on TikTok in 18 months with the same content quality. TikTok shifted to monetization mode -- they want you to pay for reach now.

You should still post on TikTok, but treat YouTube Shorts as your primary growth channel. Shorts has a unique advantage: content keeps getting views for months. On TikTok a video peaks and dies in 2-3 days. On YouTube, a Short from Week 1 can still be gaining views in Week 6.

## The one metric that matters: completion rate

Every platform's algorithm comes down to this differently, but the underlying question is the same -- did people watch the whole thing?

- TikTok: 70% completion rate is the threshold for viral distribution in 2026. Below 70%, your video almost never breaks 10K views.
- YouTube Shorts: they measure the inverse -- "swipe-away rate." Under 30% swipe-away is good.
- Instagram Reels: completion matters, but DM shares are actually the #1 signal. If someone sends your Reel to a friend, that's weighted 3-5x higher than a like.

This has a direct implication for video length. Videos under 15 seconds get 92% average completion. Videos 16-30 seconds get 84%. The math is simple -- shorter videos are easier to complete. Default to 15-25 seconds. Don't make 60-second videos unless the content genuinely demands it.

## The first 3 seconds are a separate problem from the rest of the video

71% of whether someone watches or scrolls is decided in the first 3 seconds. TikTok now has a "qualified view" threshold at 5 seconds -- your video doesn't even count as a real view until someone watches 5 seconds. So your hook needs to hold people for 5 seconds minimum.

Three things that work as hooks: pattern interruption (something visually unexpected in frame 1), curiosity gap ("wait until you see what happens"), and starting mid-action with no context. For SpaceZero specifically, the strongest hook pattern is the reverse reveal -- show the finished build first, then cut to "here's how I made it."

## SpaceZero has a natural content advantage that most games don't

Build timelapses are among the most viral formats in the sandbox game space. @team_minecraft_build gets 50K-300K+ likes per video posting nothing but build timelapses. This is SpaceZero's native content -- you don't need to invent a content strategy, you just need to record builds and compress them into 20-second clips.

From one 20-minute build recording, you can extract 5-7 unique videos: the full timelapse, a zoomed-in detail shot, a before/after, the build in reverse, a "guess what this is" at 30% completion, a real-time segment of the hardest part, and a cinematic fly-through. That's one recording session producing almost a full week of content.

## TikTok is now a search engine, and almost nobody is optimizing for it

This is the most underrated finding. People search TikTok for "best mobile games," "games like Roblox," "satisfying building game." If you say a keyword out loud, show it as text overlay, AND put it in your caption, TikTok's semantic indexing will rank you for that search term. TikTok now processes audio, reads on-screen text via computer vision, and cross-references captions -- all three layers feed into search ranking.

Most gaming creators only optimize for the For You Page (virality). Search gives you sustained, long-tail traffic that doesn't spike and die. This is where YouTube Shorts also excels -- YouTube search is much more mature, and Shorts appear alongside regular videos in search results.

## The content production math for two people

One "Content Day" per week: 3 hours recording in the morning, 3-4 hours editing in the afternoon, 1 hour scheduling. That produces 7+ unique videos. The rest of the week is 30-60 minutes per day replying to comments and monitoring. Total: 11.5-14.5 hours per week split between two people.

The editing stack is basically free: CapCut (free) for all editing and auto-captions, plus OpusClip ($19/month) when you want to auto-generate clips from longer recordings.

## The competitor lesson from Core (Manticore Games)

Core raised $100M+ to build a Roblox competitor. They never cracked the TikTok content flywheel. No breakout viral content. No user-generated content ecosystem. They pivoted away from the UGC platform model entirely. The lesson: the content engine has to be designed into the product, not bolted on. SpaceZero's in-game recording/sharing feature (ReplayKit integration) isn't a nice-to-have -- it's what makes the UGC flywheel possible. Roblox's "Clip It" feature crossed 1 billion views because sharing is built into the game.

## The conversion funnel is leakier than you'd think

Working backward from 17K installs: you need ~5M impressions. Of those, maybe 2-4% visit your profile. Of those, maybe 10-20% click a link or search the App Store. Of those, 30-50% actually install (free games convert well on the App Store). At the conservative end, 5M impressions yields 3K installs. At the optimistic end, 20K.

The two biggest leverage points aren't the content itself -- they're the App Store page and the CTA. If your App Store screenshots don't match what people saw on TikTok, they bounce. And saying "Search SpaceZero on the App Store" is better than "link in bio" because search installs also boost your App Store search ranking, creating a compounding effect.

## The Instagram difference

Instagram's algorithm is fundamentally different from TikTok and YouTube. The #1 signal is DM shares -- someone sending your Reel to a friend. This means you should design Instagram content specifically for shareability: "Send this to someone who needs this game," "Tag a friend who would build this." It's a different content psychology than TikTok (where you optimize for completion) or YouTube (where you optimize for not swiping away).

## What "good" looks like in the first 6 weeks

Weeks 1-2: most videos get 200-1K views. This is normal. The algorithm is testing your account. Don't panic.

Weeks 3-4: at least 1-2 videos should break out and hit 5K-50K views. These tell you what format the algorithm wants from your account. Double down on that format.

Weeks 5-6: you should have a clear "winning format" and be at 2K-10K followers with some videos regularly hitting 10K+ views.

If a video starts gaining traction (5K+ views in first 2 hours), drop everything and make 3-5 follow-up videos within 48 hours. That's the single highest-leverage action in the whole playbook.
