## 1. The Anatomy of a Great Creator Brief

### The golden rule

A creator brief is not a script. It is a guardrail. You are paying for someone else's voice, audience trust, and creative instincts. The brief tells them what matters to you; how they communicate it is their job.

The best sponsored content happens when creators feel like they discovered something cool and are sharing it because they want to -- not because someone paid them to read bullet points. Your brief should make that feeling possible.

### What to include

Every brief should cover these six things, and nothing else:

**1. The product in one sentence.**
Not your pitch deck. Not your vision. One sentence that tells them what the app does.

> "Zerocap is an iOS app where you point your camera at any real-world object and AI transforms it into a cute living pet with its own personality."

That is the entire product explanation. If a creator needs more than that, the product isn't ready for influencer marketing.

**2. The key moment to capture.**
What is the single most important thing viewers need to see? For Zerocap, it is always the transformation: real object on camera, then the pet appears. Everything else is decoration around that moment.

**3. 2-3 messaging guardrails (not talking points).**
These are things they must NOT say, not things they must say. Examples:
- Don't call it "augmented reality" or "AR" (confusing, clinical)
- Don't compare it to Tamagotchi directly (let the audience make that connection)
- Don't promise features that aren't live yet

**4. One CTA approach.**
How should the app mention happen? For native-feeling content, the best approach is: the creator names the app when showing the transformation, or includes it as a text overlay. No "link in bio" monologues. No "use code CREATOR20."

**5. Deliverables and timeline.**
- How many videos
- Which platform(s)
- Draft review deadline (if applicable)
- Go-live window

**6. Visual references.**
2-3 example videos that show the energy and format you want. Not Zerocap videos -- videos from other creators in the same style/format that demonstrate the vibe. "Something like this, but with our app" is more useful than any written description.

### What to leave out

- Company history or mission statement
- Market size or competitive positioning
- Feature lists longer than what the video will show
- Hashtag requirements beyond disclosure (let them use their own)
- Scripted dialogue or required talking points
- Wardrobe, setting, or background requirements (unless there is a brand safety reason)

### Brief length

One page. Ideally under 500 words. If your brief is longer than one page, you are over-directing.

Creators receive dozens of briefs. They skim. A short brief that communicates clearly will produce better content than a detailed brief that gets half-read. Put the critical information in the first three sentences. Everything else is supporting detail.

### Visual references and mood boards

Skip the formal mood board. Instead, send 2-3 links to existing videos with a one-line note on each:

> "Like this energy -- casual, surprised by the result, genuine reaction"
> "This format -- scanning multiple objects back-to-back with quick cuts"
> "This pacing -- 15 seconds, no intro, straight into the hook"

Creators understand video references immediately. Written descriptions of "aesthetic direction" are ambiguous and slow.

### The balance: direction vs. freedom

The framework is simple:

| You control | They control |
|------------|-------------|
| What the product is | How they introduce it |
| The key moment (transformation) | How they set it up |
| What NOT to say | What they DO say |
| Disclosure requirements | How they handle the disclosure |
| Deadline and deliverables | Everything else |

If you find yourself writing sentences that start with "say something like..." you are scripting, not briefing.

## 2. Content Concepts & Angles

Each concept below is designed to feel like something the creator would post on their own -- not something a brand asked them to make. The app mention should feel incidental to the content, not central to it.

### Concept 1: "I scanned my lunch and it became a pet"

**Hook (0-1.5s):** Flash-forward to the pet for 0.5 seconds, then cut to the creator holding up food. Text overlay: "my lunch is alive now"

**Content flow:** Creator shows their lunch (bonus if it is a trending food item -- see [content-concepts.md](../tiktok-gtm/content-concepts.md) for current trends). Points phone at the food. The transformation happens. Pet appears with personality traits visually tied to the food (spicy food = fiery pet, sushi = aquatic creature). Creator reacts genuinely to the pet's personality. Optional: they interact with the pet briefly (pet modifies the table/terrain around the plate).

**CTA approach:** The app name appears as a text overlay during the transformation or the creator says "this app is insane" while it happens. No formal call-to-action. The comment section does the work -- people will ask "what app is this?"

**Target platform:** TikTok (primary), cross-post to Reels and Shorts.

**Why this works:** Food content is the single largest category on TikTok. Scanning lunch is relatable (everyone eats), requires zero setup, and the before/after transformation of something mundane into something magical is exactly the curiosity gap that drives completion rates. If the food is already trending (Dave's Hot Chicken, pickle dip, boba), the video hijacks existing search traffic.

### Concept 2: "Scanning random objects in [location] to see what pets I get"

**Hook (0-2s):** Creator walking through a recognizable location (Target, IKEA, their campus, a thrift store). Text overlay: "scanning everything in [location] to build my pet army"

**Content flow:** Rapid-fire montage. Creator points phone at 4-6 different objects. Each scan produces a different pet with a quick personality caption. The pace is fast -- 2-3 seconds per object. The variety is the entertainment. End on the best/funniest pet as the payoff.

**CTA approach:** The app is visible on screen during every scan. Creator might say the app name once casually ("Zerocap keeps giving me unhinged pets") but the repeated visual of the app interface is the real driver.

**Target platform:** YouTube Shorts (this format benefits from the slightly longer format -- 30-45 seconds), cross-post shortened version to TikTok.

**Why this works:** Location-based content has built-in audience targeting (everyone who follows content about Target, IKEA, college life). The rapid-fire format provides multiple surprise moments, which increases replay value. The "collection" angle taps into the same psychology as blind box/mystery content, which performs exceptionally well on short-form video.

---

### Concept 3: "POV: your AI pet redecorated while you were gone"

**Hook (0-1s):** Camera slowly opens a door or turns a corner. Something is visibly different. Text overlay: "came home to this..."

**Content flow:** The creator "discovers" that their Zerocap pet has modified the terrain/environment while they were away. Show the pet proudly sitting in their redesigned space. The humor comes from the pet's personality driving the design choices -- a grumpy pet made everything dark and moody, a chaotic pet created an obstacle course, a fancy pet added gold everywhere. Creator reacts and interacts with the pet.

**CTA approach:** Purely organic. The app name appears naturally when the creator opens the app to check on their pet. The content is about the pet's personality, not the app.

**Target platform:** TikTok (POV format is native to TikTok's culture), Instagram Reels.

**Why this works:** POV content is one of TikTok's most durable formats. This concept leans into the "my pet has its own personality" angle, which creates emotional attachment content that viewers project onto. It also showcases the terrain modification feature -- one of Zerocap's differentiators -- without making it feel like a product demo.

---

### Concept 4: "Rating the pets I got from scanning everything on my desk"

**Hook (0-1.5s):** Top-down shot of a cluttered desk. Text overlay: "tier list of the pets from my desk items"

**Content flow:** Creator scans 5-8 desk objects one by one. After each scan, they rate the resulting pet (verbal reaction, tier ranking, or a quick score). Objects range from mundane (stapler, coffee mug) to weird (dead plant, mystery snack). The entertainment is the creator's personality shining through the ratings and the surprise of what each object produces.

**CTA approach:** App name visible during scans. Creator might end with "okay the [object] pet is top tier though" and pin a comment with the app name, or it appears as text overlay.

**Target platform:** TikTok (tier list/rating content is a proven format), cross-post to Reels.

**Why this works:** Rating and tier list content has extremely high engagement because viewers form their own opinions and argue in the comments ("the stapler pet was NOT that bad"). Desk content is relatable across demographics. The creator's personality carries the video, which means the ad feels invisible.

---

### Concept 5: Reaction/surprise format -- "I did NOT expect that"

**Hook (0-1s):** Close-up of the creator's face, clearly shocked or delighted. They are already looking at their phone screen.

**Content flow:** Cut to the phone screen showing the Zerocap transformation. Then cut back to the creator reacting. The structure alternates between the screen and the reaction 2-3 times, building comedic tension. The final pet reveal gets the biggest reaction.

**CTA approach:** The creator's genuine surprise IS the advertisement. When someone's authentic reaction to an app is delight, no CTA is needed. The app name appears on screen during the transformation. Comments will ask for it.

**Target platform:** TikTok (reaction content is algorithmically favored -- high engagement, high completion).

**Why this works:** FaceApp, Lensa, and every viral camera app was fueled by reaction content. The human reaction to a transformation is more shareable than the transformation itself. This format also works particularly well for creators who are naturally expressive -- their personality becomes inseparable from the product experience.

---

### Concept 6: "I left my pet alone for 24 hours"

**Hook (0-1.5s):** Split screen or text overlay: "24 hours ago I created a pet... I just checked on it." Dramatic music or trending audio.

**Content flow:** Creator shows the pet they created yesterday (flashback or screenshot). Then they open the app to check on it. The pet has evolved, changed mood, or modified its environment based on being "neglected." The content is the discovery of what happened -- maybe the pet is sulking, maybe it redecorated, maybe it found a friend. End with the creator interacting with the pet to make up.

**CTA approach:** The narrative structure naturally features the app multiple times (creating the pet, checking on it, interacting). No forced mention needed.

**Target platform:** TikTok, Instagram Reels.

**Why this works:** "I left X alone for [time]" is a proven viral format (fish tanks, sourdough starters, ant farms). It creates narrative tension and a natural story arc. For Zerocap, it also showcases the ongoing relationship aspect -- these are not static one-time creations, they are living things that change over time. This is the retention story told through content.

---

### Concept 7: Challenge format -- "Scan the weirdest thing you can find"

**Hook (0-1s):** Text overlay: "challenged myself to scan the weirdest things in my house." Creator holding up something absurd (a toilet plunger, a shoe, a rubber duck).

**Content flow:** Creator scans 3-5 increasingly weird objects, ranking them by how strange/funny the resulting pet is. The escalation provides comedic structure. Final object should be the most unexpected. The weirder the input, the more surprising the output -- this is a natural content engine.

**CTA approach:** End with a challenge to followers: "scan YOUR weirdest thing and show me" (creates UGC cascade). App name as text overlay.

**Target platform:** TikTok (challenge content drives participation and duets), YouTube Shorts.

**Why this works:** Challenges are TikTok's native viral mechanic. This format invites participation, which creates a content cascade. Each person who takes the challenge creates a new video that reaches their audience, extending distribution exponentially. The weirdness factor makes it entertaining even for viewers who have no interest in the app.

---

### Concept 8: Comparison format -- "Scanning expensive vs. cheap items"

**Hook (0-1.5s):** Side-by-side shot or split screen: expensive item on one side, cheap version on the other. Text overlay: "$200 vs $5 -- which makes the better pet?"

**Content flow:** Creator scans a premium item (designer sunglasses, AirPods Max, a Starbucks drink) and then the budget equivalent (gas station sunglasses, wired earbuds, instant coffee). Compare the resulting pets side by side. Does expensive stuff make better pets? The answer should be genuinely unpredictable, which is what makes it entertaining.

**CTA approach:** The repeated scanning sequences naturally show the app in action. The comparison framing makes the video about curiosity, not about the app.

**Target platform:** TikTok (vs. content is a proven format with high engagement), Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts.

**Why this works:** Comparison content has inherently high engagement because viewers take sides. "Expensive vs. cheap" is one of the most reliable content frameworks on short-form video across all categories. The subversion of expectations (cheap items potentially making better pets) adds surprise value and challenges the materialist assumption, which plays well with Gen Z values.

---

### Concept 9: Storytime format -- "The time I accidentally created the most cursed pet"

**Hook (0-1.5s):** Creator speaking directly to camera, clearly about to tell a story. "So I downloaded this app and I wasn't ready for what happened..."

**Content flow:** Creator tells a short story about their first experience with Zerocap while showing clips of the app in action. The story format allows them to add their personality, humor, and narrative pacing. The "cursed pet" angle plays into the absurdist humor that performs on TikTok -- the pet they got was so weird/ugly/funny that they had to share it. The story can also go the opposite direction: "I scanned my cat and the pet that came out looks EXACTLY like her."

**CTA approach:** The story naturally includes the app. The creator is talking about their experience, which means the app mention is woven into the narrative rather than bolted onto the end.

**Target platform:** TikTok (storytime is one of the most engaged-with formats), Instagram Reels.

**Why this works:** Storytime content builds parasocial connection. Viewers feel like they are hearing from a friend, not watching an ad. The format also allows creators maximum creative freedom -- the brief can simply say "tell us about your funniest Zerocap experience" and let them run with it. The format naturally leads to longer watch times, which the algorithm rewards.

---

### Concept 10: Duet/stitch bait -- "Scan this and show me what you get"

**Hook (0-1s):** Creator holds up a very specific, widely available object (a Coca-Cola can, a Nike shoe, a textbook). Text overlay: "scan this with Zerocap and show me yours"

**Content flow:** Creator scans the object and shows their pet. The video is deliberately short (10-15 seconds) and ends with an invitation for others to stitch/duet with their own version. The content is designed to be responded to, not just watched.

**CTA approach:** The app name is in the text overlay and the call to duet. The CTA is a social invitation, not a commercial one.

**Target platform:** TikTok (duet/stitch is native to TikTok's interaction model).

**Why this works:** Duet/stitch bait is the purest form of viral content design. Each response is a new video that reaches a new audience. If 50 creators stitch the original, that is 50 new distribution points -- all organic. The "same input, different output" comparison creates natural curiosity and debate in comment sections. This format also works as a seeding strategy: post the original from the brand account, then pay a few creators to stitch it, which triggers organic stitching from non-paid creators.

---

### Concept 11: "What pet would YOUR room give you?"

**Hook (0-1s):** Slow pan of the creator's room/apartment/dorm. Text overlay: "my room just determined my entire personality"

**Content flow:** Creator scans several objects around their room -- their bedside table, favorite hoodie, a poster, their plant. Each scan produces a pet whose personality reflects the object. The accumulation of pets tells a story about the creator's personality and aesthetic. End with a group shot of all their pets together in their modified terrain.

**CTA approach:** The video is about self-expression through the pets. The app is the tool that enables it. Natural mention, no pitch.

**Target platform:** Instagram Reels (room tour/aesthetic content performs on IG), TikTok.

**Why this works:** Room tour and "what does your room say about you" content is evergreen on both TikTok and Instagram. This concept layers Zerocap onto an existing popular format. The self-expression angle drives comments ("scan your shoes next!") and makes the content feel personal rather than promotional.

---

### Concept 12: "My pet just did WHAT" -- ongoing series

**Hook (0-1s):** Creator's face, exasperated or amused. "Update on my Zerocap pet..."

**Content flow:** A series format where the creator posts periodic updates about their pet's behavior, evolution, and terrain modifications. Each installment is a short update (15-20 seconds) covering what the pet did since the last video. The series structure creates follow incentive and positions the creator as a genuine Zerocap user, not a one-time sponsored poster.

**CTA approach:** By the second or third installment, the app name is ambient context -- viewers already know what it is. No CTA needed in ongoing episodes.

**Target platform:** TikTok (series content drives follows), YouTube Shorts.

**Why this works:** Series content has the highest follower conversion rate on TikTok because viewers follow to see future installments. For Zerocap, this format also demonstrates long-term engagement -- it shows that the app is not a one-time trick but an ongoing experience. This is valuable for combating the perception (accurate for most camera apps) that the novelty wears off quickly. Best deployed with creators who genuinely enjoy the app and will maintain the series authentically.

---

### Concept 13: Pet niche crossover -- "My real pet meets my AI pet"

**Hook (0-1s):** Real pet (dog, cat) on screen. Text overlay: "I made my dog a friend"

**Content flow:** Creator scans an object near their real pet. The AI pet appears. Creator "introduces" the real pet to the AI pet. Capture the real animal's reaction (or lack thereof) to the phone screen. The content is the juxtaposition of the real pet and the digital one, with humor coming from the real pet's indifference or curiosity.

**CTA approach:** Completely organic. The content is a pet video with Zerocap as the mechanism for the bit.

**Target platform:** All platforms (pet content is universally high-performing).

**Why this works:** Pet content is the highest-engagement category on social media (5%+ engagement rates). Combining real pets with AI pets creates a unique crossover that neither pure pet accounts nor pure tech accounts can replicate. Pet niche creators have highly loyal audiences who watch everything they post. The real pet's reaction is the wildcard that makes each video unique.

> - Whatever feels natural for your channel
>
> **What we ask:**
> - Show at least 2-3 scans so viewers see the range of outputs
> - Don't oversell -- your audience will smell it
> - You can talk about the tech if you want, but the visual transformation is what stops the scroll
>
> **What you can say that other creators can't:**
> - You can mention AI, machine learning, the tech angle -- your audience speaks that language
> - You can compare it to other AI apps if you want
> - You can critique it honestly -- if something doesn't work well, say so
>
> **Disclosure:** [PLATFORM-SPECIFIC -- see Section 5]
>
> **Deliverables:**
> - [NUMBER] video(s) on [PLATFORM]
> - Draft for review by [DATE]
> - Post within [DATE RANGE]
>
> **Compensation:** [AMOUNT]
>
> Happy to set up a call to answer technical questions if that would help your video. TestFlight link ready whenever you want it.

*Guidance notes (delete before sending):*
- *Tech creators value honesty above all. If you try to control their message, they will either refuse the deal or their audience will roast them (and you).*
- *"You can critique it honestly" is not a throwaway line. Mean it. A tech creator who says "the scan takes a bit long but the pets are genuinely cool" is 10x more credible than one who says "this app is amazing and perfect."*
- *Tech audiences are smaller but higher-intent. A 50K-follower tech creator can drive more installs than a 500K lifestyle creator because their audience actively tries apps they recommend.*
- *The "AI Tamagotchi meets PalWorld" framing works for tech audiences who understand those references. Don't use it for general audiences.*
- *Give them early access (TestFlight) well before the content deadline -- tech creators want to explore, not just demo.*

## 4. Do's and Don'ts

### What makes sponsored content feel fake vs. authentic

**Fake signals (viewers detect these instantly):**
- The creator reads off a script or uses phrases they would never normally say
- The product appears out of nowhere with no context ("speaking of productivity, have you heard of...")
- The creator lists features in a specific order (this screams "required talking points")
- The energy changes -- suddenly more polished, more enthusiastic, more careful with words
- The product gets 60 seconds of screen time in a 90-second video
- A discount code is prominently displayed

**Authentic signals:**
- The creator shows the product in the context of their normal life/content
- Their reaction to the product is proportional -- excitement where warranted, neutrality where not
- The product mention is brief relative to the total video length
- The creator uses their normal vocabulary and cadence
- They acknowledge flaws or limitations casually
- The video would still be entertaining even if you removed the product entirely

**The acid test:** Would this video make sense on the creator's feed if no money had changed hands? If yes, the brief worked. If the video looks out of place among their other content, the brief was too heavy-handed.

### Common briefing mistakes

**1. Including "key messages" or "talking points."**
The moment you give a creator three bullet points to hit, the content becomes a checklist. They will hit each point robotically, and viewers will feel it. Instead, give them one core idea: "the transformation from real object to living pet is the magic moment." Let them communicate that in their own words.

**2. Requiring specific hashtags (beyond disclosure).**
Creators know their audience better than you know their audience. Forcing #AIpet #Zerocap #sponsored #ad #virtualcompanion on a video makes it look like SEO spam. Require disclosure hashtags (#ad). Let them choose everything else.

**3. Over-specifying the setting or scenario.**
"Film in your kitchen with natural lighting, wearing casual clothes, with your phone in your right hand" -- this is directing, not briefing. Unless there is a genuine brand safety reason (don't film near competitor products, don't use the app in an inappropriate setting), let them choose where and how.

**4. Sending the brief as a PDF.**
PDFs feel corporate. They set the tone for a transactional relationship. Send the brief as plain text in a DM or email. Keep it conversational. Match the communication style to the creator's platform.

**5. Requiring multiple rounds of revision.**
One round of review is reasonable. Two rounds kills the creator's enthusiasm and often makes the content worse, not better. If the first draft needs more than minor adjustments, the brief was unclear -- that is your problem, not theirs.

**6. Not watching their content before reaching out.**
If your outreach message includes "love your content!" without referencing anything specific, the creator knows you did not watch anything. Take five minutes. Find a specific video you genuinely appreciate. Reference it. This is the difference between a creator who is excited to work with you and one who is doing it for the check.

### How much creative freedom to give by tier

| Creator Tier | Followers | Freedom Level | What You Control | What They Control |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Nano** | 1K-10K | Maximum | Product accuracy, disclosure | Everything else |
| **Micro** | 10K-100K | High | Product accuracy, disclosure, one review round | Format, setting, script, pacing |
| **Mid-tier** | 100K-500K | Moderate | Product accuracy, disclosure, concept direction, one review round | Execution, script, personality |
| **Macro** | 500K-1M | Moderate-Low | Concept, key moment, brand safety, review | Execution within agreed concept |
| **Mega** | 1M+ | Negotiated | Per contract | Per contract |

**The counterintuitive truth:** Nano and micro creators produce the most authentic content when given the most freedom. Their audiences follow them for their unpolished, genuine personality. The more you try to "professionalize" their content, the worse it performs. Mid-tier and above creators are more comfortable with structured briefs because they are used to brand partnerships and have developed a process for integrating sponsorships naturally.

### Revision process -- how to give feedback without killing the creator's voice

**Before giving feedback, ask yourself:**
1. Is this a brand safety issue? (Wrong product name, inaccurate claim, missing disclosure) -- **Give the note.**
2. Is this a creative preference? (You would have done it differently, you don't love the lighting, you wish they scanned a different object) -- **Don't give the note.**
3. Is this genuinely confusing for viewers? (The transformation is not clearly visible, the app name is inaudible, the video is so short the product is barely shown) -- **Give the note, but frame it as a viewer clarity issue, not a brand preference.**

**How to frame feedback:**
- Bad: "Can you re-film with better lighting and speak more clearly when you say the app name?"
- Good: "Looks great! One thing -- the pet is a bit hard to see at the 0:08 mark. Could you adjust so the transformation is more visible? Everything else is perfect."

- Bad: "We need you to mention that the app has 6 million trait combinations."
- Good: No. Do not send this note. No viewer cares about this number.

**The one-note rule:** If you have more than one piece of feedback, prioritize the single most important thing and send only that. Creators who receive a list of revisions feel micromanaged and produce worse content on the revision than they did on the first draft.

## 5. Disclosure Requirements

### Why disclosure matters (and why it doesn't hurt as much as you think)

There is a persistent myth in influencer marketing that disclosure kills engagement. The data does not support this. Research published in *Information Systems Research* found that FTC disclosure does not significantly harm engagement rates, and for experienced/popular creators, proper disclosure can actually improve audience trust and long-term performance.

Audiences in 2026 understand that creators make money. They do not resent sponsorships -- they resent dishonesty. A creator who says "Zerocap sponsored this video" and then genuinely has fun with the app is more trustworthy than one who pretends they just happened to discover it organically.

Disclosure protects the creator, protects Zerocap, and costs nothing in performance. Do it right every time.

### FTC guidelines (United States)

The Federal Trade Commission requires disclosure whenever a "material connection" exists between an endorser and a brand. This includes:
- Cash payment
- Free products
- Discount codes where the creator earns commission
- Any other compensation or benefit

**What the FTC requires:**
- Disclosure must use clear language: **#ad**, **#sponsored**, or "this video is sponsored by [Brand]." Vague terms like "#collab," "#ambassador," or "#spon" are NOT sufficient.
- Disclosure must appear **before the audience engages** -- not buried at the bottom of a caption or at the end of a video.
- In video content, disclosure must be **stated verbally** and/or shown as a **visible text overlay** within the first few seconds.
- Disclosure must be **unavoidable** -- it cannot require scrolling, expanding text, or clicking to find.
- Platform tools (TikTok's branded content toggle, Instagram's Paid Partnership label) **supplement** the disclosure but do NOT replace it. Always include #ad or a verbal disclosure IN ADDITION to the platform tool.

**Penalties:** Violating an FTC order can result in fines exceeding $53,000 per violation. Every non-compliant post counts as a separate violation. Both the creator AND the brand can be held liable.

**For Zerocap briefs, always include this line:**
> "Include #ad in your caption and use the platform's built-in disclosure tool (details below). This protects both of us."

### Platform-specific disclosure tools

#### TikTok

**Required actions:**
1. Enable the **Content Disclosure Setting** when posting. This adds a "Paid partnership" or "Promotional content" label to the video.
2. Include **#ad** in the caption text.

**How it works:** When posting, creators select "Content Disclosure" and toggle on "Branded content." The video will display a "Paid partnership" label. If the creator is promoting their own brand, it shows "Promotional content" instead.

**Enforcement:** TikTok actively detects undisclosed branded content. Videos flagged as potentially commercial but not properly disclosed receive a notification. Creators have 24 hours to add disclosure before the video becomes ineligible for the For You feed. Non-compliant videos get suppressed algorithmically.

**For your brief, include:**
> "Before posting: go to Content Disclosure in your video settings and turn on 'Branded content.' Also include #ad in your caption."

#### Instagram

**Required actions:**
1. Use the **Paid Partnership** label (tag Zerocap as the brand partner).
2. Include **#ad** in the caption **above the fold** (before the "...more" cutoff).

**How it works:** When creating a Reel or post, creators tap "Add paid partnership label" and tag the brand partner. This adds a "Paid partnership with [Brand]" label at the top of the post.

**Important:** The Paid Partnership label alone does NOT satisfy FTC requirements. Always require #ad in the caption text as well. Technical issues can sometimes hide the native label; the hashtag serves as a backup.

**For your brief, include:**
> "Use Instagram's Paid Partnership label (tag @zerocap) and include #ad at the start of your caption -- before the 'more' cutoff."

#### YouTube / YouTube Shorts

**Required actions:**
1. Check the **"My video contains paid promotion"** box in video details.
2. Include **#ad** in the video title OR the first line of the description.
3. Give a **verbal disclosure** within the first 15-30 seconds of the video.

**How it works:** YouTube displays a "Includes paid promotion" overlay for the first 10 seconds of the video when the paid promotion box is checked.

**For your brief, include:**
> "In your video details, check the 'paid promotion' box. Include #ad in your title or first line of description. Give a quick verbal mention ('this is sponsored by Zerocap') in the first 15-30 seconds."

### How to handle disclosure without killing the vibe

The goal is compliance that feels natural. Here are approaches that work:

**The casual verbal mention (best for TikTok and YouTube Shorts):**
> "Zerocap sent me this app and I can't stop scanning things"

This is a disclosure (identifies the material connection) that doubles as a content hook. It is compliant AND sounds like something a person would actually say.

**The text overlay (best for fast-paced content):**
A small "#ad" text overlay in the first 1-2 seconds, placed where it is visible but not the focal point. Viewers register it, accept it, and move on. It costs you zero engagement.

**The pinned comment (supplementary, not primary):**
After posting, the creator pins a comment: "Partnered with Zerocap for this video! The app is [App Store link]." This provides information for interested viewers without cluttering the video itself. This is a supplement, NOT a replacement for in-content disclosure.

**What to avoid:**
- Long sponsorship disclaimers that break the flow ("Before we get into the video, I want to thank my sponsor Zerocap. Zerocap is an incredible app that...")
- Hiding #ad in a wall of hashtags at the end of the caption
- Assuming the platform's built-in tool is sufficient without additional disclosure
- Telling creators to skip disclosure ("just don't make it too obvious") -- this is illegal and puts both parties at risk

### Quick reference: disclosure checklist per platform

| Platform | Platform Tool | Caption/Description | Verbal | Additional |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|--------|-----------|
| **TikTok** | Content Disclosure toggle (Branded content) | #ad in caption | Recommended | -- |
| **Instagram Reels** | Paid Partnership label | #ad above the fold | Optional | -- |
| **Instagram Stories** | Paid Partnership label | #ad as text overlay | N/A | Use text overlay since no caption |
| **YouTube Shorts** | Paid promotion checkbox | #ad in title or first description line | Required (first 15-30 sec) | -- |
| **YouTube (long-form)** | Paid promotion checkbox | #ad in description | Required (first 30 sec) | Verbal + visual recommended |

## Appendix: Campaign Planning Notes

### Budget allocation by creator tier

For a "buy native videos" approach where the goal is volume of authentic content (not reach per post), the most efficient allocation is:

| Tier | Cost Range (per video) | Expected Views | Cost per 1K Views | Best For |
|------|----------------------|----------------|-------------------|----------|
| Nano (1K-10K) | $50-$200 | 2K-20K | $5-$25 | Volume, authenticity, testing concepts |
| Micro (10K-100K) | $200-$1,000 | 10K-100K | $5-$15 | Targeted reach, niche audiences |
| Mid (100K-500K) | $1,000-$5,000 | 50K-500K | $5-$15 | Scale with quality |
| Macro (500K+) | $5,000-$25,000 | 200K-2M+ | $5-$15 | Ignition moments |

Nano and micro creators offer the best combination of authenticity and cost efficiency for an early-stage app. Start there. Use macro creators strategically for ignition moments, not as the default.

### Outreach sequence

1. **Find creators** who already post content adjacent to Zerocap's world (cute content, AI experiments, pet content, food content, reaction content).
2. **Watch 5-10 of their videos** to understand their voice and format.
3. **Send a short, personalized DM or email.** Reference a specific video. Keep it under 100 words.
4. **If they respond positively,** send the appropriate template brief.
5. **If they ask questions,** answer quickly. Speed signals professionalism.
6. **Send TestFlight access** so they can play with the app before committing to a concept.
7. **After content is posted,** engage with it immediately (like, comment from brand account). Amplify through your own channels.

### Measuring success

The primary metric for native creator content is **installs attributed to the video**, not views or engagement. Set up:
- Unique App Store links or UTM parameters per creator
- Post-install event tracking (first scan, first pet created, Day 1 retention)
- A simple spreadsheet tracking: Creator, Platform, Views, Installs, Cost per Install, Day 7 Retention of those installs

Secondary metrics worth tracking:
- Comment sentiment (are people asking "what app is this?" -- that means the content worked)
- Organic stitch/duet activity (did other creators respond to the video unprompted?)
- Search volume lift for "Zerocap" on the App Store after posting
