## 1. Philosophy: community first, promotion second

Discord is not a billboard. It is a living room. You are a guest in someone else's house.

The entire strategy rests on one principle: **you must be a person people are glad to see in chat before you are ever a person with a product to share.** If you skip this, nothing else in this document works. You will get banned, ignored, or worse — build a reputation as a spammer that follows your brand across servers.

### Why this works on Discord specifically

Discord has 259 million monthly active users spending an average of 94 minutes per day on the platform — more than Instagram (30-40 min) or TikTok (20 min). But unlike those platforms, Discord is conversational. There is no algorithm boosting your content to strangers. Reach comes from trust, and trust comes from showing up consistently and being useful.

The math is different here than on TikTok. On TikTok, one viral video from a zero-follower account can drive 100K downloads. On Discord, one genuine relationship with a server owner can unlock an announcement to 10,000 engaged members who actually read it. Lower ceiling, higher conversion rate, more durable.

### The mindset

- You are a **member** of these communities, not a marketer visiting them.
- You bring **expertise** (AI, app development, virtual pets, design) before you bring a product.
- You share your product only when it's **genuinely relevant** to a conversation someone else started.
- Your goal is to be the person that, when they do share something, people actually click because they trust you.

This is relationship-based marketing. It is slower to start and harder to fake, which is exactly why it works.

### How to evaluate a server before investing time

Not all servers are worth your time. Before committing to active participation, assess these factors:

**Activity ratio (most important):**
- A server with 10,000 members and 5% activity (500 active) is worth less than one with 2,000 members and 40% activity (800 active).
- Check: scroll through the main channels. Are messages flowing? Are they from today? Are multiple people talking, or is it one person monologuing?
- Benchmark: aim for servers where at least 10-15% of members are active monthly. The best communities hit 50%+.

**Message quality:**
- Are people having real conversations or just posting memes and leaving?
- Do questions get answered? Do people help each other?
- High-quality discussion = high-quality audience = higher conversion if/when you share your product.

**Demographics fit:**
- Does the server's audience overlap with Zerocap's target? (Cute content lovers, virtual pet enthusiasts, AI early adopters, iOS users, Gen Z / young millennials)
- Look at the language, topics, and cultural tone. A server full of hardcore PC gamers discussing frame rates is probably low-value for Zerocap.

**Rules on promotion:**
- **Read the rules channel first.** Every server has one. Look for:
  - Is there a #self-promo or #showcase channel? (Green light for eventually sharing)
  - Are links allowed in general chat? (Usually no)
  - What are the consequences for rule violations? (Some servers auto-ban, others warn)
  - Is there a minimum participation threshold before posting in promo channels? (Some require a certain role or time in the server)

**Moderation quality:**
- Well-moderated servers have higher-quality members and more trust. They're harder to promote in but far more valuable when you do.
- Poorly moderated servers are full of spam and bots — your promotion will be noise among noise.

**Verification level:**
- Servers that require phone verification, role assignment, or an introduction post tend to have more genuine members.

## 3. Engagement strategy on other servers

### Phase 1: Silent observation (Days 1-3)

Before posting a single message:

- **Read the rules.** Fully. Not skimming. Know what's allowed and what will get you kicked.
- **Read the last 2-3 days of conversation** in the main channels. Understand the tone, the inside jokes, the norms.
- **Identify the regulars.** Who are the most active members? Who are the mods? Who are the respected voices?
- **Note the channel structure.** Which channels are active? Which are dead? Is there a #showcase, #self-promo, #what-are-you-working-on, or #introductions channel?

### Phase 2: Genuine participation (Days 3-14)

The goal for this phase: **become a recognized name without ever mentioning Zerocap.**

What to do:
- **Answer questions.** If someone asks about AI, virtual pets, app development, or anything you know — help them. Detailed, useful answers. Not one-liners.
- **React to messages.** Use emoji reactions. It signals presence without being noisy.
- **Join conversations naturally.** "Oh that's cool, I've been playing with [related thing] too" — be a person, not a brand.
- **Share your expertise freely.** If you know about AI image generation, iOS development, the history of Tamagotchi, pet content on TikTok — share that knowledge. Be the person who adds value when they speak.
- **Compliment other people's work.** If someone shares a project in #showcase, give real feedback. Not "looks cool!" but "the animation on the idle state is really smooth, how are you handling the sprite sheets?"
- **Participate in events.** If the server hosts game nights, art contests, group activities — show up. This builds social capital faster than anything.

What NOT to do in this phase:
- Mention your app or link to it
- Set your Discord status to "download Zerocap!"
- Use a username or avatar that's blatantly promotional
- DM anyone about your product

### Phase 3: Soft introduction (Days 14-21)

After 2+ weeks of genuine participation, you have earned the right to be known as someone who is building something. The transition should feel natural, not staged.

**Natural conversation entry points:**

- **"What are you working on?" threads** — Many developer and creative servers have weekly or monthly threads where members share their projects. This is the single best opportunity. Share Zerocap as what you're building, not what you're selling. Show a screenshot or short video. Talk about the challenge of generating unique personalities, not the download link.

- **When someone asks a related question** — "Has anyone seen a good AI pet app?" / "What happened to Tamagotchi?" / "Are there any cool AR/AI camera apps?" — Now you can answer honestly: "Actually, I'm building one. Here's what it does." This is not promotional. This is answering a question.

- **Responding to show-and-tell with your own** — After someone shares their project and you give them feedback, it's natural to share yours: "Love what you did with [X]. I'm working on something in a similar space — it's an app that transforms real-world objects into AI pets. Here's a quick clip."

- **Reacting to related news** — If someone shares an article about AI companions, virtual pets making a comeback, or the Tamagotchi brand: "This is exactly why I've been obsessed with this space. I'm actually building [brief description]."

**The key distinction:** You are sharing what you're working on as a fellow community member, not pitching a product as a marketer. The energy is completely different. People can feel it.

### Phase 4: Established member (Day 21+)

Once you are a recognized, trusted member of a community:

- You can share updates about Zerocap's development naturally ("shipped a new feature today — pets now react to weather")
- People will ask you about your project unprompted
- You can post in #self-promo or #showcase channels without it feeling like spam because people know you
- Other members may share your app for you ("hey check out what [your username] is building, it's actually cool")

This last point — **organic advocacy from other community members** — is the gold standard. It is worth more than 100 self-promotional posts.

### Timeline summary

| Phase | Duration | Goal | Zerocap mentions |
|-------|----------|------|-----------------|
| Silent observation | Days 1-3 | Understand the community | Zero |
| Genuine participation | Days 3-14 | Become a recognized, helpful member | Zero |
| Soft introduction | Days 14-21 | Naturally share what you're building | Only in context, no links |
| Established member | Day 21+ | Be a trusted voice who happens to build a cool app | Natural, in-context, links when appropriate |

## 4. Promotion tactics that don't get you banned

Every tactic below assumes you have already done Phase 1-3 above. If you skip straight to these, you will be treated as a spammer regardless of how well-crafted your message is.

### 4.1 Designated self-promo channels

Most well-structured Discord servers have a channel specifically for members to share their work. Common names:
- `#self-promo` / `#self-promotion`
- `#showcase` / `#show-your-work`
- `#shameless-plug`
- `#cool-stuff` / `#interesting-finds`
- `#devlog` / `#what-are-you-building`

**How to post in self-promo channels effectively:**

Do:
- Lead with what makes Zerocap interesting, not with "download my app"
- Include a screenshot or short video clip — visual content gets 5-10x more engagement than text-only posts
- Explain what's unique: "This app transforms real-world objects into AI pets using your camera. Every pet gets a unique personality from 6M trait combinations"
- Mention that you're an indie team of 4 — people root for small teams
- End with a low-pressure CTA: "If anyone wants to try it, it's on the App Store" (not "DOWNLOAD NOW")
- Share genuine development milestones, not just "app is live"

Don't:
- Copy-paste the same post across 20 servers
- Post more than once per week in any self-promo channel
- Use ALL CAPS or excessive emojis
- Include multiple links (one link to the App Store is enough)

**Example self-promo post:**

```
Hey all — been lurking here for a while, wanted to share something I've been
building with my team.

It's called Zerocap — you point your phone camera at any object and AI
transforms it into a cute living pet with its own personality. Your coffee mug
might become a shy, jazz-loving creature. Your shoe might become an overly
confident adventurer.

We're a team of 4 and just launched on iOS. Here's a quick clip of what the
transformation looks like: [video/gif]

Would love to hear what you think. And if anyone wants to try it, it's free
on the App Store: [link]
```

### 4.2 Responding to "looking for app recommendations" threads

These appear regularly in tech, lifestyle, and general servers:
- "What are some cool new apps?"
- "Any good AI apps worth trying?"
- "What's a fun app you discovered recently?"
- "Recommendations for [anything tangentially related]?"

When these appear, you can respond honestly. The key is to not be the first response (looks staged) and to frame it as a genuine recommendation alongside other things:

```
I've been using [other app] for [purpose], it's great. Also been obsessed with
Zerocap lately — it's an AI tamagotchi where you scan real objects with your
camera and they turn into pets. Full disclosure, I'm one of the people building
it, but genuinely think you'd like it if you're into [relevant interest].
```

The "full disclosure" transparency is critical. Hiding that you're the developer and getting caught = permanent credibility loss.

### 4.3 Devlog-style updates that people find interesting

Developer communities especially love process content — not "check out my product" but "here's the interesting technical/design challenge I faced this week."

**Devlog formats that work on Discord:**

| Format | Example | Why it works |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| Technical challenge | "We spent a week figuring out how to make AI-generated pets feel alive with just subtle idle animations. Here's the before/after." | People learn something. They engage with the problem, not the pitch. |
| Design decision | "Should AI pets die like original Tamagotchis, or live forever? We went back and forth. Here's why we chose [X]." | Sparks discussion. People have opinions. |
| User surprise | "Someone scanned a fire hydrant and got a firefighter-personality pet. We didn't program that — the AI inferred it from the shape and color." | Delightful and unexpected. Shows the product without selling it. |
| Milestone | "We just hit [X] pets created. The most common object people scan is [Y]. Nobody expected that." | Data is interesting. People share interesting data. |
| Behind-the-scenes | "Here's what Zerocap looked like 3 months ago vs. today. The personality system used to be 50 traits, now it's 6 million combinations." | Growth stories are compelling. |

**Cadence:** One devlog-style post per community per week, maximum. More than that and it starts feeling like a content calendar, not a person sharing.

### 4.4 Visual content that sells itself

Zerocap has a massive advantage over most apps trying to do Discord GTM: **the output is inherently visual and shareable.** A before/after of a coffee mug transforming into a cute AI pet is interesting even if you never download the app.

- Share transformation clips in general chat when the conversation is about AI, creativity, or cute things — "speaking of AI doing cool stuff, look what happened when I scanned my [object]"
- Post pet screenshots in art/aesthetic channels where visual content is welcome
- React to conversations about pets, AI art, or Tamagotchi nostalgia with relevant Zerocap clips

The content does the selling. You just provide context.

This is the highest-impact tactic and requires the most trust (which is why it's last). See Section 5 for the full playbook.

## 5. Partnership with server owners

Server owners and moderators are the gatekeepers. Their endorsement is worth more than 100 self-promotional posts because their members trust them. A single announcement from a server owner to 10,000 members will outperform weeks of individual engagement.

### How to build the relationship

1. **Be a great community member first.** Server owners notice who contributes. If you've been helpful, answered questions, participated in events, and followed rules — they know your name.

2. **Engage with the owner/mods directly.** Not about your product. About the community. "Love what you've done with the [event/channel/feature]. How did you get the idea for [X]?" Genuine interest in their work.

3. **Help the community in ways that benefit the owner.** Offer to help moderate, contribute to a FAQ, help answer common questions. This builds social capital that no amount of self-promotion can buy.

4. **Wait for the right moment.** Don't pitch a partnership in your first week. Wait until they've seen your name enough times that it feels like a peer conversation, not a cold pitch.

### How to propose a partnership

Once you have a relationship, the approach depends on the server type:

**For developer/creator communities:**

```
Hey [owner name], I've been a member here for a while and really enjoy the
community. I'm building an app called Zerocap — an AI Tamagotchi where you
scan real objects and they become living pets.

I'd love to do a little showcase or demo for the server if you think members
would find it interesting. I could do a live demo in voice, share the dev story,
and answer questions about the tech behind it. Totally up to you — I don't want
to overstep. Just thought it might be a fun addition.
```

**For virtual pet/nostalgia communities:**

```
Hey [owner name], love what you've built here. The [specific event/feature]
is genuinely one of my favorite things about this server.

I'm part of a small team building Zerocap — it's basically an AI Tamagotchi
where you point your camera at real objects and AI turns them into living pets
with unique personalities. We're huge virtual pet nerds ourselves (that's why
I'm here).

Would you be open to me doing a little giveaway or showcase for the community?
I could offer [X] to members. No pressure at all — just thought your community
would genuinely enjoy it.
```

### Giveaways and exclusive content for server members

Giveaways are one of the most effective partnership formats because they give the server owner something to offer their community:

| Giveaway type | What you offer | What it costs you | Impact |
|---------------|---------------|-------------------|--------|
| **Early access** | TestFlight or beta access exclusively for server members | Nothing (if pre-launch) | Creates exclusivity, generates feedback, builds word-of-mouth |
| **Exclusive pets** | A special pet design or trait only available to members of that server | Dev time for one asset | Strong incentive, collectibility drives downloads |
| **Feature unlock** | Premium feature access for free (time-limited) | Deferred revenue | Lower friction to try the app |
| **Art contest** | "Scan something weird and share the pet. Best transformation wins [prize]" | Small prize ($25 gift card, merch) | Generates UGC, makes the server owner look good for hosting a fun event |
| **Group event** | "Let's all scan the same object and compare what pets we get" | Nothing | Social, fun, generates discussion |

**The value exchange:** You get access to the server owner's audience. The server owner gets free content/events for their community. Both sides benefit. Frame it that way.

### Co-hosted events

Events create concentrated moments of engagement that drive installs in bursts:

| Event type | Format | Duration | Expected impact |
|------------|--------|----------|----------------|
| **Live demo in voice chat** | You screen-share Zerocap, scan objects live, show how it works. Members suggest objects to scan. | 30-60 min | High engagement, immediate downloads from participants |
| **"Scan battle" tournament** | Members scan objects and post their pets. Community votes on the best/funniest/cutest. | 24-48 hours | UGC generation, keeps conversation going for days |
| **AMA (Ask Me Anything)** | Team answers questions about building an AI pet app, the tech, the journey. | 1-2 hours | Builds brand, shows the humans behind the product |
| **Themed scan challenge** | "Scan only things in your kitchen" / "Scan the weirdest thing you can find" | 1 week | Ongoing engagement, multiple touchpoints |
| **Devlog Q&A** | Share a development update, then open the floor for questions. | 30-45 min | Developer communities especially love this format |

**Logistics for co-hosted events:**
- Coordinate with the server owner on timing (their community knows when peak hours are)
- Let the server owner make the announcement (their voice, their authority)
- Prepare materials in advance (screenshots, video clips, talking points)
- Be available to answer questions for at least 1-2 hours after the event
- Follow up with a thank-you to the owner and a recap post in the server

## 6. What NOT to do

These are not just "bad practices" — they are actions that will get you banned, damage Zerocap's reputation across communities, and potentially get your Discord account disabled or permanently banned by Discord itself.

### DM spamming members

**Discord's ToS explicitly prohibits sending unsolicited marketing messages.** This is not a gray area.

Consequences:
- The recipient reports you → Discord Trust & Safety investigates
- First offense: warning from Discord + possible quarantine (banned from messaging or joining new servers)
- Repeat offense: account disabled, then deleted
- Severe/repeated offense: **permanent ban + phone number blacklisted** from all future Discord accounts
- Server-level: instant ban from whatever server the recipient is in
- Reputation: word travels fast. Server owners talk to each other. One DM spam report can get you preemptively banned from multiple communities.

**Even "friendly" unsolicited DMs about your product are spam.** "Hey, I noticed you like virtual pets — want to check out something cool?" is still a cold DM. Don't do it.

The only acceptable DM scenario: someone asks you about your product in a public channel and you continue the conversation in DMs at their request.

### Dropping links without context

Posting a bare App Store link (or even "Check out Zerocap! [link]") in any channel that isn't explicitly for self-promotion:
- Gets auto-deleted by most bots (many servers have link filters)
- Gets you warned or muted by moderators
- Makes you look like a bot, not a person
- Trains the community to ignore everything you say in the future

### Join-promote-leave (hit and run)

Joining a server, immediately posting your promotion in general chat, and leaving (or going silent) is the single most common mistake indie developers make on Discord. It is also the least effective and most damaging approach.

- Moderators flag and ban these accounts instantly
- Some servers use bots that detect new members who post links within their first few messages and auto-ban them
- Your server join activity is visible — if a moderator checks and sees you joined 50 servers in one day, you're marked as a spammer
- Other members in the server see the ban and associate your product name with spam

### Violating server rules

Every server has rules. Common rules you might accidentally violate:
- **No links in general chat** — Even if you're sharing something relevant, check if links are allowed outside of specific channels
- **No self-promotion outside of #self-promo** — Even a casual mention of "my app" in general chat can trigger this rule in strict servers
- **Minimum role/time requirement to post** — Some servers require you to have a certain role (earned through activity) before posting in certain channels
- **No duplicate/cross-posted content** — Don't post the same message in multiple channels within the same server
- **English only** — Some servers enforce language rules

**Read the rules. Follow them. No exceptions.** The first impression you make by violating a rule is nearly impossible to recover from.

### Using alt accounts to amplify

Creating multiple accounts to make your product look popular ("Wow, Zerocap is awesome!" from alt #1, "Just downloaded, love it!" from alt #2) is:
- Against Discord's ToS
- Incredibly obvious to moderators
- An instant permanent ban if caught
- Genuinely pathetic — people will screenshot it and mock it publicly

### Automating engagement with bots

Using bots to auto-join servers, auto-react, or auto-post is detectable and will result in account termination. Discord's anti-spam systems specifically target this behavior.

## 7. Tracking results

### Attribution challenge

Discord → App Store install is one of the hardest attribution paths in mobile marketing because:
- Discord links don't carry UTM parameters through the App Store
- iOS privacy restrictions (App Tracking Transparency) limit post-install attribution
- Users often see something on Discord, search the App Store later, and install — with no trackable connection

### Practical tracking methods

Despite the challenges, here's how to measure which servers are driving installs:

**1. Unique landing pages / link shorteners per server**

Create a unique shortened URL for each server you're active in:
- `zerocap.link/tamagotchi-collectors` → App Store page
- `zerocap.link/indie-dev` → App Store page
- `zerocap.link/midjourney` → App Store page

Use a service like Bitly, Short.io, or your own domain with redirect tracking. This tells you which links get clicked, even if you can't perfectly attribute installs.

**2. Promo codes per server**

If Zerocap has any premium features or in-app content:
- Create a unique promo code for each server (e.g., `TAMACOLLECTORS`, `INDIEDEV`)
- Track redemptions to know where users came from
- This is the most accurate method because the user self-identifies their source

**3. "How did you hear about us?" in onboarding**

Add a simple question during onboarding: "Where did you discover Zerocap?"
- Options: TikTok, Instagram, Reddit, Discord, App Store, Friend, Other
- If "Discord" — optionally ask which server
- Low-friction (one tap), high-value data

**4. Spike correlation**

Track daily install numbers. When you do a server event, partnership announcement, or post in a self-promo channel, note the date and time. Look for install spikes that correlate with your Discord activity.

| Date | Server | Action | Installs that day | Baseline | Delta |
|------|--------|--------|-------------------|----------|-------|
| Example | Tamagotchi Collectors | AMA + giveaway | 45 | 12 | +33 |
| Example | Indie Games | Devlog post | 18 | 12 | +6 |

**5. Discord-specific onboarding flow**

If you have your own Discord server, track how many new members join from other servers:
- When someone joins your server, you can see their mutual servers (if public)
- Ask in #introductions: "What brought you here?"
- People who say "saw you in [server name]" = attributed

### Metrics to track

| Metric | What it tells you | How to measure |
|--------|-------------------|---------------|
| Click-through rate per server link | Interest level per community | Link shortener analytics |
| Installs per server event | Impact of specific actions | Spike correlation + promo codes |
| Server-attributed promo code redemptions | Definitive server-to-install attribution | In-app analytics |
| "How did you hear about us?" responses | Self-reported source | Onboarding survey |
| Time from server join to first Zerocap mention | How long to build credibility per server type | Manual tracking |
| Messages sent before first product mention | Engagement investment per server | Manual tracking |
| Server ban rate | Whether your approach is working or too aggressive | Should be 0% |

## 8. Time investment: realistic schedule for a 4-person team

### The uncomfortable truth

Being an indie dev is 40% development and 60% marketing and business. Discord GTM on other people's servers is labor-intensive because it requires genuine human engagement — you cannot automate authenticity.

One practical case study from a solo indie developer: joining 15 Discord servers, spending 1-2 days just helping people and answering questions, then mentioning their tool in context. Result: ~60-70 downloads out of 196 total, with real user conversations that shaped the next version. Discord provided roughly 30-35% of all downloads despite being one of many channels.

### Recommended allocation

For a 4-person team, Discord outreach should be one person's secondary responsibility, not four people's side task. Spreading across the whole team creates inconsistency — the same person needs to build relationships in the same servers.

**Assign one team member as the Discord lead.** Ideally someone who:
- Actually enjoys hanging out in Discord communities (this shows)
- Has knowledge to share (dev, design, AI, virtual pet culture)
- Is patient enough to spend 2+ weeks helping people before mentioning Zerocap
- Can write conversationally, not in marketing-speak

### Weekly time budget: Discord lead

| Activity | Time/week | Notes |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| **Daily check-ins across 5-8 active servers** | 3-4 hours | 20-30 min per server. Read recent messages, react, answer questions, participate in conversations. |
| **Devlog/showcase posts** | 1 hour | Prepare 1-2 quality posts per week for self-promo channels across servers. Not copy-pasted — tailored to each community. |
| **Server owner relationship building** | 1 hour | DMs with owners/mods, discussing partnerships, coordinating events. |
| **Event preparation and execution** | 1-2 hours | When running co-hosted events (not every week). Prep materials, run the event, follow up. |
| **New server scouting and evaluation** | 30 min | Check Disboard/Top.gg for new relevant communities, evaluate, join 1-2 per week. |
| **Tracking and reporting** | 30 min | Update tracking spreadsheet, note which servers are driving results, flag opportunities. |
| **Total** | **6-9 hours/week** | ~1 hour/day on average, with some days heavier than others |

### The ramp-up period (Weeks 1-4)

The first month is the highest time investment with the lowest visible return. This is the trust-building phase.

| Week | Servers joined | Time spent | Zerocap mentions | Expected installs |
|------|---------------|------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Join 5-8 servers, observe | 5-6 hours | 0 | 0 |
| 2 | Begin participating in all servers | 6-8 hours | 0 | 0 |
| 3 | Start soft introductions in 2-3 servers where you're most established | 6-8 hours | 2-3 natural mentions | 5-15 |
| 4 | Active participation + first self-promo posts in designated channels | 6-8 hours | 5-8 posts/mentions | 15-40 |

**Total month 1: ~25-30 hours invested, 20-55 installs expected.**

This is not impressive on paper. The value compounds. By month 2, you have credibility in 5-8 servers, relationships with 2-3 server owners, and the ability to do things (partnerships, events, announcements) that drive 10-50x more installs per action.

### Steady-state (Month 2+)

The other 3 team members don't need to be active in external servers, but they should:
- Be available for AMAs or technical questions during events (30 min/month each)

## 9. Scaling without it becoming a full-time job

### The diminishing returns problem

There is a natural limit to how many external Discord servers one person can meaningfully participate in. The math:

- Each server requires ~20-30 minutes per day to stay current and contribute
- At 5 servers: 2-2.5 hours/day (sustainable)
- At 10 servers: 4-5 hours/day (the entire morning, every day)
- At 15 servers: 6-7.5 hours/day (this IS a full-time job)
- At 20+ servers: impossible to maintain quality. You become a lurker in most, which defeats the purpose.

**The sweet spot is 5-8 servers with deep engagement, not 20 servers with shallow presence.**

### Scaling strategies that don't require more time

**1. Prioritize ruthlessly**

After month 1, you'll have data on which servers are actually driving results. Double down on the top 3 and drop the bottom 3. Not every community is worth your time — and that's fine.

| Server | Time invested | Installs attributed | Installs per hour | Keep? |
|--------|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|-------|
| Tamagotchi Collectors | 8 hrs | 35 | 4.4 | Yes — expand |
| Indie Dev Network | 6 hrs | 12 | 2.0 | Yes — maintain |
| Random tech server | 5 hrs | 2 | 0.4 | No — drop |

**2. Convert external engagement into owned community growth**

The ultimate scaling move: bring people from other servers into your own Zerocap Discord. Once someone is in your server:
- You can reach them with announcements (no more navigating other people's rules)
- They become advocates who share in their other communities
- They provide feedback that improves the product
- The community grows itself

Every time you interact with someone on an external server, your Discord link should be in your profile. Not pushy — just available.

**3. Leverage content across servers**

A devlog post written for one server can be adapted (not copy-pasted) for 2-3 other servers:
- Tamagotchi server version: emphasize the virtual pet angle, nostalgia
- Dev server version: emphasize the technical challenge, architecture decisions
- AI server version: emphasize the AI generation pipeline, model choices

Same story, different framing. 1 hour of prep → 3 posts across 3 servers.

**4. Build "ambassador" relationships**

After building relationships in 2-3 servers, identify 1-2 members in each who genuinely like Zerocap and would naturally talk about it. These are your unpaid, unasked ambassadors:
- Give them early access to new features
- Ask for their feedback (making them feel like insiders)
- Thank them publicly when they share the app
- They do the ongoing engagement in those communities while you focus elsewhere

This is not a formal "ambassador program" — that feels corporate and kills the authenticity. This is just treating your biggest fans well and staying in touch.

**5. Automate the trackable parts only**

You cannot automate the human engagement. But you can automate:
- Link tracking (use a dashboard, not manual spreadsheets)
- Install spike alerts (set up notifications when daily installs exceed baseline)
- Content scheduling (prepare devlog posts in advance, post them at optimal times)
- New server alerts (set up Google Alerts or Disboard notifications for new servers in your categories)

**6. Create evergreen content**

Some content keeps working without ongoing effort:
- A well-written pinned post in a #showcase channel that new members discover for months
- A FAQ or guide you contributed to a community that links to Zerocap naturally
- A memorable event recap that people reference when the topic comes up again

### The endgame: diminishing need for external promotion

If Zerocap succeeds, the flywheel eventually flips:
1. Your own Discord grows → you spend more time there, less on external servers
2. Users advocate for Zerocap in their own communities without you asking
3. Server owners approach you for partnerships (not the other way around)
4. You shift from "person promoting in other communities" to "community worth partnering with"

The goal of this entire strategy is to reach the point where you no longer need it.

## Appendix: Quick-reference rules
